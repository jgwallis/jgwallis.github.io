Good value and always reasonable

Current charges

From 6th April 2017, my basic rate for computer troubleshooting is £28 per hour with a minimum chargeable time of one hour. After the first hour, time is charged in quarter-hour chunks, with any part of a quarter-hour counting as a whole quarter-hour. So one-and-a-quarter hours of work is £35, one hour and twenty-five minutes is £42, and so on. This rate is very competitive, being considerably lower than many other professionals would charge. However, as the rate has been been held at the same level for several years it will probably have to go up at some time before 5th April 2018 to take account of inflation.

There’s no call-out fee for local work. but there’s a half-hour charge (£14) for any job that’s more than five miles from my home as the crow flies. I have to charge this to account for the time it takes to drive both ways, fuel, business insurance, and sometimes parking as well. However, most computer support businesses always charge a call-out fee, and the only way you can avoid it is by taking your problem to them, which is often inconvenient.

Charges for tuition are considerably lower, because teaching is easier and far less stressful than fixing problems. Please get in touch for further details.

Please note that there is no Value Added Tax on top of these charges.

No fix, no fee

If I’m called out to solve a specific problem and find that I can’t fix it, I don’t charge anything at all. If, however, I’m called out to solve a problem and am successful, but am then asked to look at another problem that I can’t solve, I still charge for fixing the initial problem.

‘No fix, no fee’ means that if I can’t solve a problem then I won’t charge. However, if a potential customer doesn’t give me a chance to fix whatever problem I’ve found then there’ll usually be a small diagnosis fee (equivalent to half-an-hour’s time). I had to introduce this policy shortly after starting my business because sometimes a potential customer would call me out to discover what was wrong with their computer, then decide to not bother fixing it, or to fix it themselves, or to get somebody else to fix it. I ended up doing a lot of diagnostic work and not getting paid a penny.

Being reasonable

If you call me out to do a job and it turns out to be very quick and simple, I’ll only ask for a small, reasonable payment. In other words I never stick rigidly to my normal charges if doing so would be unfair. Also, if a job is likely to go on for a very long time, I will almost always be willing to cap the charge at a mutually agreeable maximum. A lot of my work is repeat business for customers who appreciate my reasonable approach to charging, so you can call me out without worrying about how much it’s going to cost. In fact, when customers ask how much they owe me and I tell them, they often say ‘is that all?’

Payment

I accept payment in cash, by cheque, or by direct bank transfer (Bacs, which used to be known as BACS (the Bankers’ Automated Clearing Services). Unfortunately I don’t have the facility to process credit or debit card payments, as the overhead would be too much just to handle a small number of transactions.

Invoices

For regular domestic customers, and businesses, I’m happy to operate on a credit basis, where I issue a paper or electronic invoice to be paid within twenty-eight days. Of course I am always glad to provide an invoice to any customer who wants one for their records, though I am unable to provide them retrospectively (I have occasionally been asked to provide invoices literally months after the relevant events).
